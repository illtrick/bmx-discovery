The work, briefly

Two interviews. Two roles. One product to learn from.

Customer-led discovery is the practice of starting with what customers actually do and say, not what we assume about them, and letting that shape product decisions. This brief discovery work demonstrates what even two well-chosen interviews can surface.

Each conversation was a scheduled 30 minutes, recorded with consent. AI was used to transcribe, surface patterns, create this site and provide draft copy. I orchestrated the tools then improved clarity and focus. One participant is a developer; one is a resident. Both live in Seattle.

To make this drive business decisions

Here's what would make it a foundation.

With another week and access to additional users, I would close three specific gaps before treating these findings as anything more than directional:

  • Property Management. The Operator's account suggests Property Management drove the recommendation in his case. The Resident side gives us no read on this group at all. Understanding the value props and pain points of this group is imperative for validation and developing any respective strategy. The Operator specifically offered to connect me with the new Property Management group at his building once a transition completes.
  • An installer or low-voltage integrator. The Operator uses one for installation and post-warranty service but wasn't able to connect me to them. ButterflyMX has publicly emphasized installer-friendliness as a wedge — the absence of in-unit wiring, the ease of deployment. The installer perspective is the one most likely to surface implementation friction the Operator never sees.
  • A deep dive on installation data and alignment to ButterflyMX's strategic goals. I would analytically develop a baseline for the real-world application of the product portfolio as a starting place, then build the roadmap to take us to the "top of the mountain."
Finding 01

The Operator made the call. But the decision lived in conversation with Property Management.

The Operator has the capital, the entitlements, the design authority. He's the one signing the contract.

But asked how he chose ButterflyMX, his answer wasn't about features or specs. It was about who he asked first.

Frankly, we reached out to our property managers and just said, "What are you guys using? What's the most effective for you?" It came down to the fact that our property managers were familiar with Butterfly, and that was pretty widely used in the Seattle market.
Eric H. · Operator

The Operator made the call. But before he made it, he asked Property Management what they wanted.

The Operator's account points to Property Management groups — working with the same vendors across competing buildings — as an influential voice in the buying decision, and potentially a deal blocker if they won't support a product. A single operator's experience doesn't establish this as the category pattern, but it's directionally consistent with how this channel tends to operate. More interviews with operators and integrators across markets would sharpen the picture considerably.

Implication for product
If the pattern holds across more interviews, the operator dashboard becomes more than a feature — it becomes a structural advantage. Workflow depth, training, certification, day-to-day reliability for Property Management groups would compound across every building in every market. Worth pressure-testing how much further this channel can be widened relative to where competitors are investing.
Finding 02

Both customers asked for the same thing, in different words.

The Operator volunteered it twice, unprompted. The Resident echoed it without ever having heard the Operator say it. With only two conversations, it's hard to call any finding definitive — but this is the most resonant convergence the two interviews produced.

The Operator's framing
There's still this kind of fragmentation, and I think that's true in the market. We're still kind of piece-mealing systems together and they work well enough together. But it feels like there's room there for somebody to figure that out.
Whoever can break into that space first to say "here's all-inclusive" — I'm sure there is somebody that's done it, I don't know if anybody's done it well enough where it's fully adopted.
Eric H. · Operator
The Resident's framing
If Butterfly was looking to do something new or expanded, it would be sweet if it was all just one app.
Basically right now I have a Butterfly app, a Package Concierge app that I never use, and the resident experience app which I never use because it's so brutal to work with. I'd rather use a website.
Ted C. · Resident

The Operator is asking for a unified back-of-house: one vendor, one contract, one support call. The Resident is asking for a unified front-of-house: one app, one place to look, one fewer thing to download.

Same gap. Different ends of the same product. The Operator voice surfacing operational fragmentation. The Resident voice surfacing cognitive fragmentation. Two perspectives, independently named — a stronger signal than either alone, though still two data points.

Implication for product
This is a platform decision — build, partner, or acquire — not a line on a roadmap. What's striking is how much of the hard part ButterflyMX has already done: the unit-door locks, the property-management software, even the leasing AI already connect to it. The pieces are there, but they don't yet feel like one product — one system the operator runs, one app the resident actually opens. That's the opportunity these two interviews point to, and the package room shows it most plainly: for this resident, it's still a separate app and a separate login. The question worth testing with more conversations is how much of that experience ButterflyMX should own outright versus stitch together from the partners it already has.
Finding 03

Users value the app's simplicity and reliability — but there's opportunity to help them even more.

The Resident's account of living with the ButterflyMX app is, in the simplest terms, restrained praise. He doesn't think about it much. It doesn't get in his way. It does what he needs it to do. He sent the screenshot below unprompted near the end of the interview, while walking through his actual usage.

Visitor Pass / Delivery Pass
Top of screen. Most prominent affordances. "I know the app offers guest passes you can give people in advance. I've never really used that." — Ted C., Resident
Lobby Ramp, Main Entrance, P1 Garage
The Resident enters these with a physical fob, not the app. The fob is just faster.
Package Room
The only door the Resident regularly opens with the app. ~95% of his app usage.

In his own words, the Resident called the app "fast enough and nice enough that I don't think it adds drag to the experience." That's restrained praise — but it's praise. The product gets out of his way, and his usage pattern reflects that confidence.

The opportunity is in what sits just behind the simplicity. The same Resident who finds the basics frictionless also revealed real confusion about how parts of the system work — specifically the elevator integration:

The only thing that I wasn't clear on initially was how it interfaces with the elevators, but I was happy to learn — I'm still basically just guessing here how this works. It seems like when you call me from the panel outside, I can unlock the door with my phone and then the system must be tied into the elevators and it basically unlocks my floor for a period of time, is what I'm guessing.
Ted C. · Resident

And about which doors and readers are ButterflyMX versus other systems in the building:

I almost wonder if that's part of a Butterfly system. The fob taps on the non-Butterfly panel reader thing and the door opens. Alternatively, I could open my app and make the door open with Butterfly, but I can't do that for my own apartment unit.
Ted C. · Resident
Implication for product
The app is working. Be sure any change clears the ROI bar before shipping. A clearer near-term opportunity may be in-app help — an AI assistant that can answer "will my guest be able to use the elevator?" or "how do I give the dog walker scheduled access?" using the building's actual configuration. The Resident's confusion isn't about the controls. It's about understanding the system. Worth testing whether other residents describe the same gap.
Finding 04

For the Resident, the package experience dominates in-app use — and points toward a clear expansion opportunity.

95% of the Resident's ButterflyMX engagement happens at the Package Room door. It's the only door he can't open with his fob. So when he gets a package, he opens the app.

What happens after he opens it is where the experience falls apart — and almost none of it is ButterflyMX's fault directly.

The package guy — random, he's got like 20 packages — he randomly picks one person's package and says "I'm here for Tim." He goes in there and puts 20 different packages in. Tim gets a notification and no one else does.
When it gets overcrowded, the system breaks down, and then you spend like 10 minutes digging around trying to find your package.
Ted C. · Resident · on the package room workflow

But the more interesting signal is what the Resident does not use ButterflyMX for. The property also has a parcel locker system from Package Concierge that delivers a meaningfully better experience for individual packages:

The Package Concierge machine is where I prefer all of my packages to go because my package gets an individual cubby that's locked, and they have their own app and you register your phone number with them. Anytime someone puts a package in the Package Concierge, I get a text and a barcode, and I go down and I scan my barcode and the thing pops open. That's the single nicest thing in this area of services that I have encountered.
Ted C. · Resident

The locker only fails because it can't keep up with the building's volume — overflow lands in the ButterflyMX-controlled room, where the dysfunction starts.

Importantly, the Resident barely uses the Package Concierge app either. Once registered, the experience runs on text-and-barcode. He has three apps installed for one building, uses one of them, and prefers a workflow that bypasses the apps entirely.

Implication for product
From this single resident's experience, the more promising path looks like integration over net-new hardware — ButterflyMX as the unified identity and notification layer, the locker as a hardware partner, no separate registration, no second app. That direction is worth pressure-testing with more residents, with Property Management operators, and against ButterflyMX's existing partner roadmap.
Finding 05

A proposed framework for where AI belongs in the product. Context is critical.

AI in access and security is high-stakes territory. Done well it compounds operator efficiency, reseller reach, and resident experience. Done poorly it accelerates the surveillance critique this category has been navigating for years and damages resident trust where trust is the product.

What follows is a priority-ordered framework for introducing AI to a security and access control product — drawn from these two interviews and from industry knowledge of where adjacent platforms have moved. Not a definitive position. A starting point for a conversation worth having.

01 Highest leverage
Operator insights and efficiency gains

If the pattern holds more broadly, Property Management is a critical channel — and AI that makes their job faster, sharper, and more confident is a high-leverage investment. The most concrete near-term version is AI layered on ButterflyMX's Security Cameras — anomaly surfacing, searchable footage, and sharper package-room identification that turns passive video into the few moments an operator actually acts on. Largely greenfield in this category. Plausible direct lift on retention.

Console management · automated reports · suggested automations · anomaly surfacing · package room organization
02 Channel multiplier
Tools for resellers and installers

The Operator uses a low-voltage consultant for install and post-warranty service. He couldn't remember their name. The installer is invisible to the Operator but central to the product's actual deployment. AI that lets a reseller quote, configure, and install ButterflyMX faster compounds across every job they win.

Automated site surveys · quote generation from building plans · install troubleshooting copilot · field service knowledge base
03 Resident experience — non-security
Make the non-security use cases easier for residents

The Resident described real confusion about how the elevator integration works and which doors are ButterflyMX versus other systems. In-app help that can actually answer "how do I give the dog walker scheduled access?" using the building's real configuration is a resident-facing AI play that adds value without crossing any sensitive lines. Same for package handoff, visitor flow education, and onboarding.

In-app help and guidance · package handoff workflows · visitor flow walkthroughs · onboarding for new residents
04 Highest caution
Use cases that alter access and security itself

Facial recognition at the door, chatbot-handled visitor approval, algorithmic permissioning that bypasses human judgment. The Resident's resistance here was the most articulate position he took in the interview — and he isn't anti-AI in general:

If someone's coming to get into the building on my behalf, I prefer to have to approve it myself. I don't want some bot to have any authority over that. There's a lot of opportunity for mistakes.
Ted C. · Resident

The cost of getting this wrong is asymmetric — surveillance critique damages the category, not just one feature. Move slowly. Keep humans in the loop. The test: does this AI make a decision a resident would otherwise make themselves?

Implication for product
The line isn't between "AI" and "no AI." It's about where AI augments operators and residents versus where AI starts making access decisions for them. Compound the first; move with discipline on the second.
